Abstract 1

Understanding speech in noisy environments relies strongly on binaural cues such as 2

interaural time and level differences (ITDs and ILDs), which support spatial hearing and the 3

segregation of competing sound sources. When these cues are degraded, listeners experience 4

substantial difficulty in complex acoustic environments. 5

Behavioural measures of binaural benefit , such as binaural masking level differences , binaural 6

intelligibility level differences , and spatial release from masking (SRM) , are well established in 7

normal -hearing (NH) listeners, but they require an active behavioural response. Neural speech 8

tracking using electroencephalography (EEG) has emerged as a promising approach for 9

quantifying neural  processing of continuous speech, yet its sensitivity to spatial hearing cues 10 

remains insufficiently characterised. 11 

We investigated the neural correlates of spatial release from masking in NH listeners using 12 

EEG -based neural speech tracking. Nineteen participants listened to continuous Dutch speech 13 

stories presented with masking noise under two spatial configurations , collocated (S0N0) and 14 

spatially separated (S0N90) , across multiple signal -to -noise ratios (SNRs). Neural tracking of 15 

the speech envelope was quantified using both envelope reconstruction and temporal 16 

response function (TRF) analyses. 17 

Spatial separation enhanced neural tracking of the target speech envelope, particularly at 18 

challenging SNRs where behavioural SRM was also observed. TRF analysis further revealed 19 

increased amplitudes and decreased latenc ies  of late cortical components consistent with 20 

spatial unmasking effects. 21 

These findings demonstrate that neural speech tracking captures cortical signatures of spatial 22 

unmasking and closely reflects behavioural improvements in speech understanding. 23 

Establishing these relationships in NH listeners supports the development of objective neural 24 

measures for evaluating binaural benefit in difficult -to -test populations. 25

Introducti on 27 

Spatial hearing —the ability to perceive, localize, and segregate sounds based on their spatial 28 

origin —is a fundamental function of the auditory system. It relies on the neural processing of 29 

binaural cues such as interaural time differences (ITDs) and interaural level differences (ILDs), 30 

together with monaural spectral cues shaped by the head and pinnae (Blauert, 1997) . Through 31 

the integration of these cues, spatial hearing supports the formation of auditory objects and 32 

enables the parsing of complex acoustic scenes, particularly in natural, multi -source listening 33 

environments (Blauert, 1997; Litovsky et al., 2021) .34 

A central functional role of spatial hearing lies in its contribution to speech understanding in 35 

everyday listening conditions. Speech communication rarely occurs in quiet; instead, listeners 36 

must typically extract a target talker from background noise or competing speakers. Spatial 37 

separation between sound sources allows the auditory system to exploit binaural unmasking 38 

and spatial release from masking, thereby improving access to the target speech signal and 39 

facilitating selective auditory attention (Dieudonné & Francart, 2019; Litovsky et al., 2021; 40 

Plomp, 1976) . These benefits reflect not only peripheral acoustic advantages but also central 41 

and cortical mechanisms that integrate spatial and linguistic information over time (Li et al., 42 

2024; Zobel et al., 2022) .43 

The behavioural advantages of spatial hearing for speech perception are well established. 44 

Improved speech intelligibility with spatially separated sources has been demonstrated across 45 

a wide range of acoustic conditions, including varying signal -to -noise ratios (SNRs) and 46 

masker types (Best et al., 2012; Biberger & Ewert, 2019; Blauert, 1997; Litovsky et al., 2021) .47 

At the neural level, electrophysiological studies have shown that cortical activity more strongly 48 

tracks attended than unattended speech, particularly when spatial cues support perceptual 49 

segregation (Das et al., 2018; Hausfeld et al., 2020; Kerlin et al., 2010) . Together, these 50 

findings point to a close relationship between binaural hearing, selective attention, and cortical 51 

speech processing. 52 

In parallel, electrophysiological approaches have been used to investigate neural sensitivity to 53 

binaural cues, employing event -related potentials (ERPs), frequency -following responses 54 

(FFRs), auditory steady -state responses (ASSRs), and auditory change complexes (ACCs). 55 

These studies have targeted specific mechanisms such as interaural phase differences, 56 

binaural interaction components, and envelope -based cues (e.g., Clinard et al., 2016; Fan & 57 

Gifford, 2024; Gransier et al., 2017; Haywood et al., 2015; Laumen et al., 2016; McAlpine et 58 

al., 2016; Undurraga et al., 2016; Van Eeckhoutte et al., 2018; Vercammen et al., 2017) . While 59 

these electrophysiological approaches provide valuable insight into binaural cue encoding 60 

under controlled conditions, they often rely on simplified stimuli and probe isolated cue 61 

processing rather than continuous, ecologically valid speech perception. Establishing how 62

More recently, neural speech tracking paradigms have emerged as a powerful tool for studying 66 

auditory processing under naturalistic listening conditions. Using electroencephalography 67 

(EEG) combined with system identification techniques such as temporal response functions 68 

(TRFs), the brain can be modelled as a linear or quasi -linear system that transforms continuous 69 

speech features —most commonly the speech envelope —into time -locked neural responses 70 

(Crosse et al., 2016, 2021) . Both encoding (forward/TRF) and decoding (backward/envelope 71 

reconstruction) approaches have demonstrated that neural tracking strength correlates with 72 

speech intelligibility (Vanthornhout et al., 2018) , attentional focus and listening effort (Decruy 73 

et al., 2020; He et al., 2025; Lesenfants & Francart, 2020) , and can be robustly measured in 74 

cochlear implant users as well (Somers, Verschueren, et al., 2018; Verschueren et al., 2019) .75 

Importantly, neural speech tracking has also begun to be applied to research spatial and 76 

binaura l hearing.  Previous work has reported spatial modulation of TRFs and envelope 77 

reconstruction accuracy in multi -talker environments (Bednar et al., 2017; Bednar & Lalor, 78 

2018) , speech -in -noise paradigms with manipulated binaural cues (Dieudonné et al., 2024) ,79 

and extensions across developmental populations and competing -speech conditions (Ahmed 80 

et al., 2026; Song et al., 2020) . These studie s, however , have not specifically examined how 81 

spatial separation and signal -to -noise ratio relate to neural speech tracking of continuous, 82 

natural speech in normal -hearing listeners. 83 

Characterising this relationship is an important step toward a mechanistic understanding of 84 

spatial hearing at the cortical level and toward  objective neural markers grounded in normal 85 

auditory processing.  In the present study, we therefore focus exclusively on normal -hearing 86 

listeners and investigate how spatial hearing manipulations —specifically spatial separation 87 

and variations in signal -to -noise ratio —affect EEG -based neural tracking of continuous, natural 88 

speech . By studying these factors in a normal auditory system, this work takes a step toward 89 

a neurophysiological reference framework for interpreting spatial hearing effects in more 90 

complex or clinical populations .91 

Signal -to -noise ratio was systematically varied across spatial listening conditions . We92 

hypothesi sed that spatially driven enhancements in neural speech tracking would be most 93 

pronounced at challenging SNRs. Under these conditions, spatial separation is expected to 94 

yield measurable increases in envelope reconstruction accuracy, reflecting spatial release from 95 

masking at the cortical level. In addition, we (specifically ) hypothesi sed that spatial unmasking 96 

would be reflected in the morphology of the TRFs, with increased amplitude s and de creased 97 

latenc ies for spatially separated compared to collocated conditions, particularly at low SNRs. 98 

This prediction is motivated by previous auditory evoked potential and envelope tracking 99

Methods 108 

Participants 109 

Nineteen adults (1 7 female, median age 20 years, min = 19, max=33 ) participated in the study. 110 

All participants were native Dutch speakers with  pure -tone hearing  thresholds at or below 20 111 

dB HL at all octave audiometric frequencies from 250 to 8000 Hz. This study was approved by 112 

the Medical Ethics Committee Research UZ/KU Leuven (project number S70169 ). All 113 

participants gave written informed consent for their participation in the s tudy. 114

Experimental set -up 116 

The experiment took place in a double -walled  soundproof  room . Stimuli were presented 117 

through Genelec loudspeakers (Genelec Oy, Iisalmi, Finland) positioned approximately one 118 

met re away from the centre of the head of the participant at 0° and 90° or −90° azimuth. Stimuli 119 

were presented via an RME Fireface UCX soundcard (Audio AG, Haimhausen, Germany), 120 

using the software platform APEX 4 (Francart et al., 2008) . We recorded EEG using a BioSemi 121 

ActiveTwo system (Amsterdam, The Netherlands), with 64 active electrodes, at a sampling 122 

frequency of 8192 Hz. 123 

124 

Stimuli 125 

Target speech wa s always presented from a loudspeaker located in front of the participant (0° 126 

azimuth). Stationary speech -weighted noise was presented either from the same frontal 127 

loudspeaker (0° azimuth , referred to as collocated ) or from a lateral loudspeaker (90° or −90° 128 

azimuth , referred to as separated ), creating collocated and spatially separated masking 129 

conditions. The position of the masker in the separated condition was kept constant for the 130 

duration of the session. The mask ing noise spectrum was derived from the long -term average 131 

spectrum (LTAS S) of the speech material.  Target speech was presented at 55 dB A, and the 132 

signal -to -noise ratio (SNR) was manipulated by adjusting the masker level. 133

Procedure 136 

Participants completed t wo parts . In the first one, purely behavioural, they completed an 137 

adaptive speech -in -noise procedure using the Flemish Matrix Sentence Test (Luts et al., 2014) .138 

The test consists of lists of 20 sentences spoken by a female speaker. Each sentence lasts 139 

approximately 2 s and follows a fixed grammatical structure (name, verb, numeral, adjective, 140 

object). The adaptive procedure started at −4 dB SNR and after each sentence the participants 141 

selected the words they heard from a matrix displayed on a screen in front of them. The SNR 142 

was adapted based on the number of correctly identified words according to the procedure 143 

described by Brand and Kollmeier (2002) .144 

145 

In the second part participants listened to fragments of stories in Dutch while we recorded EEG 146 

responses . Fragments were presented in trials lasting 2.5 –5 min. SNR  was set to −13, −10, 147 

−8, −6, −4, and −1 dB and remained constant throughout each trial. These levels were 148 

determined in pilot testing to capture a wide range of performance while avoiding ceiling and 149 

floor effects. Trials were presented in pairs, with both trials in a pair sharing the same SNR. 150 

Within each pair, the order of the two spatial conditions (separated and co llocated) was 151 

random ised. The sequence in which the six SNRs were presented was likewise randomi sed. 152 

In total, e ach participant listened to 5–7.5 minutes of material for each unique combination of 153 

SNR and speaker loc ation. Three participants completed single trials of approximately 5 min 154 

per SNR and spatial condition (≈60 min EEG total). Two participants completed two trials of 155 

2.5 min each per SNR and spatial condition (≈60 min EEG total). The remaining 13 participants 156 

completed three trials of 2.5 min each (≈90 min EEG total). Due to technical reasons data 157 

collection failed for  one participant at −1 dB SNR;  therefore, data for this condition are reported 158 

for n = 18 , whereas all other SNR conditions include data from n = 19 participants. 159 

160 

Behavioural Measures 161 

Speech reception thresholds (SRT) were obtained using the Flemish Matrix Sentence Test 162 

(Luts et al., 2014) . The SRT corresponded to the signal -to -noise ratio (SNR) at which 163 

participants correctly identified 50% of the target words and was estimated from the final SNR 164 

of the adaptive track (Brand & Kollmeier, 2002) . Spatial release from masking was quantified 165 

as the difference in SRT (in dB) between the separated and collocated masking conditions. 166 

To complement the behavioural SRT measure, subjective speech understanding was 167 

assessed after each story trial. Participants were asked to estimate the percentage of the story 168 

they understood by answering the question: "How m any of the  words  did you understand (in 169 

percent)?" 170

EEG Preprocessing and Envelope Extraction 173 

EEG signals were digitized at 8192 Hz and initially high -pass filtered at 0.1 Hz. The data were 174 

then downsampled to 512 Hz. Eyeblinks and other artifacts were automatically removed using 175 

a Multi -Channel Wiener Filter (Somers, Francart, et al., 2018) . Subsequently, the EEG signal 176 

was further downsampled to 128 Hz to reduce computational load. 177 

Channels were band -pass filtered between 0.5 and 8 Hz using a linear -phase FIR filter ,178 

implemented as separately designed high - and low -pass filters. Each filter was designed with 179 

the least -squares method to achieve a stopband attenuation of at least 10 dB and a stopband 180 

ripple below 0.25 dB, and the resulting group delay was compensated for so that no phase 181 

distortion was introduced . Signals were subsequently re -referenced to the average of all 182 

channels and normali sed so that each channel ha d zero mean and unit standard deviation .183 

Trials corresponding to the same SNR × spatial location combination were concatenated for 184 

further analysis. 185 

The speech stimuli were processed through a 28 -channel gammatone filter bank with 186 

equivalent rectangular bandwidth  spacing. The envelope of each frequency band was obtained 187 

by ta king its absolute value and then compressing it using a 0.6 power law  (Biesmans et al., 188 

2015). Like the EEG, the envelopes were band -pass filtered between 0.5 and 8 Hz, normali sed, 189 

and trials corresponding to the same SNR × spatial location combination were concatenated. 190 

The resulting signals were then normalised for subsequent neural tracking analyses. 191

For the forward model, we combined the data in the way described for the backward model. 245

After calculating the TRFs for each electrode (per participant, per condition), we applied a 250 

spatial filter to match the polarity of the TRFs over electrodes following the method applied by 251 

Dieudonné et al. (2024) . The filter was estimated in two steps. First, reconstruction accuracy 252 

was computed per electrode in a cross -validated way , independently for each subject and 253 

condition. Second, to estimate a single, common spatial filter, we identified the electrode with 254 

the highest average reconstruction accuracy across all subjects and conditions. For each 255 

remaining electrode, we computed the correlation between its average TRF and the TRF of 256 

this reference electrode. If this correlation was negative, the sign of that electrode's TRFs was 257 

flipped for all subjects and all conditions. 258 

The resulting filte r, with coefficients of +1 or −1 , was then applied uniformly across all subjects 259 

and conditions by averaging over all polarity -matched electrodes, yielding one TRF per subject 260 

per condition. 261

Peak analysis 263 

Latencies showing consistent effects across subjects were identified using cluster -based 264 

permutation testing. At each time point, TRF values across subjects were tested against zero 265 

using a cluster -based permutation test (5000 permutations) with sign -flipping across subjects. 266 

Consecutive samples exceeding a cluster -forming threshold (cluster α = 0.01) were grouped 267 

into clusters, and cluster significance was evaluated using the maximum cluster -sum statistic. 268 

Clusters surviving the corrected significance threshold ( α = 0.025, two -tailed) were considered 269 

significant . Time window showing significant effects that were preserved across condition pairs 270 

were selected for further analysis (Gillis et al., 2021; Maris & Oostenveld, 2007) .271 

Within each selected time window, we aimed to characterize the dominant TRF deflection. 272 

When a clearly defined local extremum was present, the dominant peak was identified as the 273 

local maximum (for positive components) or minimum (for negative components) within the 274 

window of interest. To improve the temporal resolution of peak latency estimates 275 

(approximately 8 ms at a sampling rate of 128 Hz), a parabola was fitted through the extremum 276 

and its two neighbouring samples, and peak amplitude and latency were defined as the 277 

amplitude and timing of the extremum of the fitted parabola. 278

Adaptive SRT estimation 338

Backward model results 374 

Envelope tracking was obtained for each of the 12 SNR x spatial -location combinations, 375 

leading to 226 values (2 conditions × 6 SNRs × 19 subjects; one subject contributed 10 rather 376 

than 12 values due to a missing SNR) . Significant envelope tracking was defined as correlation 377 

values exceeding a 95% significance threshold derived from a permutation -based null 378 

distribution. This null distribution was obtained by correlating the original pre -processed 379 

speech envelope with circularly time -shifted versions of itself, thereby preserving the temporal 380 

structure and autocorrelation of the signals, estimated from 1,000 resamplings. This procedure 381 

was performed independently for each of the 12 combinations within each subject. Only 5 out 382 

of the 226 neural tracking values fell below this threshold. All five were in the collocated 383 

condition, at −13 dB SNR (n = 2) and −10 dB SNR (n = 3). 384

TRF results 443 

Cluster -based permutation testing revealed significant TRF response clusters across 444 

conditions, with the most consistent effects across SNRs occurring between approximately 445 

150 ms and 250 ms. These clusters motivated the focus on this latency range, which 446 

corresponds to the standard mid -latency TRF components.  Figure 3A shows the average 447 

shape of the TRFs across subjects for each tested condition as well as the latencies in which 448 

the cluster permutation test showed a consistent deflection . In addition, increasing SNR was 449 

associated with more consistent responses both at early latencies following stimulus onset 450 

(40 –60 ms) and at later latencies around 300 ms. 451 

Peak amplitude and latency were operationally defined as the maximum (for positive 452 

components) or minimum (for negative components) TRF value within predefined time 453 

windows of interest corresponding to the standard components P1, N1, P2, and N2 (see 454 

Methods) . These window -based extrema were extracted for each subject and condition and 455 

entered into linear mixed -effects models  with SNR and spatial condition ( collocated vs. 456 

separated) as fixed effects and subject as a random intercept, explicitly acknowledging that for 457 

some components the extracted value may not reflect a clearly identifiable physiological peak. 458 

SNR = −1 dB was excluded to include all subjects (n = 1 9). 459 

All four components showed significant amplitude growth with SNR (P1: p < 0.001; N1: p = 460 

0.018; P2: p < 0.001; N2: p < 0.001). P2 and N2 latencies decreased significantly with SNR 461 

(P2: p = 0.002; N2: p = 0.030), while P1 and N1 latencies were SNR -invariant. Regarding 462 

spatial condition, P1 showed greater amplitude (p = 0.017) and later latency (p = 0.007) in the 463 

separated condition, and P2 peaked earlier when noise was separated (p < 0.001). N1 and N2 464 

were insensitive to spatial condition in both amplitude and latency. A significant SNR × location 465 

interaction was found only for P2 amplitude (p = 0.040), indicating attenuated SNR -dependent 466 

amplitude growth in the separated condition. 467 

Post -hoc analyses were deliberately restricted to components exhibiting consistent 468 

morphology across subjects. Accordingly, pairwise comparisons were performed only for the 469 

P2 component, which was the only peak that could be reliably identified across all subjects 470 

and conditions. For the remaining components, peak identification was inconsistent, and the 471 

window -based extrema should be interpreted as descriptive rather than reflecting robust, 472 

physiologically distinct peaks.  Supplementary Figure 2 therefore reports amplitudes and 473 

latencies for P1, N1, P2, and N2 obtained using the window -based extrema approach, 474 

illustrating that only P2 exhibits consistent and interpretable trends across conditions. No 475 

further pairwise analyses were conducted for P1, N1, or N2. 476 

Pairwise comparisons across SNRs for the P2 component revealed a significant decrease in 477 

latency with spatial separation for all conditions except −13 and −1 dB  as shown in  Figure 3C.478

Behavioural results and spatial release from masking 492 

The present study confirmed that SRM is robustly observed in normal -hearing listeners. 493 

Participants showed decreased speech reception thresholds when target and masker were 494 

spatially separated, with an average SRM of 6. 7 dB as measured in the adaptive procedure .495 

Th ese results also agree with the self -reported percentage of understood words for the stories: 496 

as expected, the largest benefits occurred at the lowest SNRs , highlighting the SNR -497 

dependent nature of binaural unmasking. Subtle asymmetries between left - and right -side 498 

maskers may reflect potential right -ear  advantage effects or small acoustic asymmetries in the 499 

experimental setup, consistent with prior behavioural findings (Garadat et al., 2009; Williges et 500 

al., 2015) . Across all tested SNRs, self -reported speech understanding was consistently higher 501 

in the separated condition, with effect sizes strongest at the lowest SNRs, emphasi sing the 502 

functional importance of spatial cues in challenging listening conditions. 503 

504 

Neural envelope tracking 505 

Building on these behavioural observations, backward -model analyses show that spatial 506 

separation enhances cortical speech tracking, especially at low SNRs (−13 to −6 dB), with the 507 

SNR –tracking function shifted toward lower SNRs in the spatially separated condi tion . Notably, 508 

these effects were observed in  17 out of 19  subject s at intermediate low SNRs, suggesting 509 

the potential subject -specific  reliability  of neural tracking measures as indicators of spatial 510 

unmasking. These results suggest that neural SRM parallels behavioural SRM, highlighting 511 

the potential value of envelope tracking as an objective marker of spatial unmasking. In 512 

contrast, previous work by Song et al. (2020) reported no overall effect of spatial separation 513 

on envelope tracking. In their study, which investigated the effects of masker type , spatial 514 

location and language experience, the collocated and separated conditions were presented at 515 

different SNRs in order to equate behavioural performance, which may explain the absence of 516 

a spatial -separation effect. By testing across a broad SNR range (−13 to −1 dB) under 517 

energetic masking, we consistently observed neural benefits at challenging SNRs, revealing 518 

spatial unmasking effects that narrower designs may miss. These findings align with 519 

Dieudonné et al. (2024) , who reported increased neural tracking linked to binaural release from 520 

masking, and with Ahmed et al.  (2026) who found that adults show stronger track ing of target 521 

speech when competing talkers were spatially separated . Converging evidence comes from 522 

EEG -based auditory attention -detection paradigms, in which decoding accuracy calculated 523 

from envelope tracking improves as competing talkers are more separated in space (Das et 524 

al., 2018) . Collectively, these studies suggest that spatial separation enhances neural tracking 525 

under adverse listening conditions,  and that this neural benefit reflects the speech -526 

understanding gains associated with spatial hearing. 527

TRFs and cortical dynamics 528 

TRF analyses revealed condition -dependent modulation of both timing and amplitude, with 529 

prominent deflections between 150 –250 ms corresponding to canonical N1 –P2 –N2 530 

components. P2 amplitude and latency emerged as particularly sensitive to spatial 531 

configuration and SNR, showing larger amplitudes and earlier latencies in separated, low -SNR 532 

conditions. At higher SNRs, the clusters in which the TRF differed from zero across subjects 533 

(i.e. the time windows surviving the permutation threshold) were more extensive in both spatial 534 

conditions. The relatively short recording durations per condition likely contributed to inter -535 

subject variability and may have limited sensitivity to more subtle spatial effects , including the 536 

resolution of individual peaks ; extending recording times and increasing the number of trials 537 

would improve reliability and allow for better separation of early sensory versus later integrative 538 

components (Crosse et al., 2021) .539 

Spatial distribution analyses demonstrated central electrode activity increases with spatial 540 

separation, consistent with engagement of broader cortical networks . Such redistribution 541 

parallels findings from ERP and ACC studies, indicating recruitment of additional cortical 542 

resources under complex listening scenarios. These results support the interpretation of TRFs 543 

as continuous -speech counterparts of classical AEPs, capturing hierarchical processing from 544 

early acoustic encoding to later integrative stages sensitive to spatial unmasking  (Dieudonné 545 

et al., 2024; Kim & Epp, 2023; Li et al., 2024; Zobel et al., 2022) . The observed TRF patterns 546 

align with literature on cortical auditory evoked potentials, where spatial separation increases 547 

P2 amplitudes, shortens latencies, and reflects perceptually relevant unmasking (Fan & 548 

Gifford, 2024; Papesh et al., 2017) . TRFs extend this framework to continuous speech, with 549 

later components such as P2 showing sensitivity to speech intelligibility beyond purely acoustic 550 

representation. Binaural unmasking effects observed in TRFs, such as decreased latencies 551 

and increased P2 amplitudes, mirror findings from comparable neural -tracking and evoked -552 

potential  studies under both low SNR and masked conditions (Dieudonné et al., 2024; Kim & 553 

Epp, 2023; Zobel et al., 2022) , suggesting that TRFs are sens itive to the same effects as those 554 

measures . Consistent with these TRF observations, earlier studies suggest that binaural 555 

unmasking arises primarily at cortical stages.  Brainstem ASSRs show minimal BMLDs, 556 

whereas cortical ASSRs and FFRs reflect sensitivity to binaural cues and perceptual SRM 557 

(Clinard et al., 2016; Koerner et al., 2020; Wong & Stapells, 2004) . The present results 558 

reinforce this hierarchy, as spatial separation enhanced cortical tracking selectively under 559 

adverse conditions rather than producing uniform gains across the auditory pathway, indicating 560 

the potential role of higher -level cortical mechanisms in exploiting spatial cues.  It should be 561 

noted, however, that brainstem responses are generally harder to record than cortical 562 

responses (Bidelman et al., 2018; Maddox & Lee, 2018) ; the observation that binaural -563

difference in measurement sensitivity rather than from a true cortical origin of the effects. 565

Implications for difficult -to -test populations 567 

The convergence of behavioural and neural SRM supports the use of neural tracking as a 568 

flexible and objective framework for assessing spatial hearing. An objective measure is 569 

especially valuable for populations that are difficult to test behaviourally, such as young 570 

children or listeners who cannot provide reliable responses, where neural tracking can probe 571 

how auditory pathways transmit spatial cues without requiring an overt behavioural task . By 572 

establishing normative patterns in normal -hearing listeners, these findings provide 573 

benchmarks for evaluating both cortical processing and binaural benefits in clinical and device -574 

based interventions  (Balkenhol et al., 2020; Dieudonné et al., 2024; Gillis et al., 2022; Sasaki 575 

et al., 2009) . Building on the observed correspondence between behavioural and neural SRM, 576 

beyond TRFs, neural tracking metrics could be used to assess continuous speech processing 577 

under varying SNRs and spatial configurations, capturing unmasking, redundancy, and 578 

squelch effects, and may serve as a useful tool for evaluating binaural listening and informing 579 

hearing -device assessments. Employing more diverse acoustic scenarios and SNRs could 580 

further elucidate how cortical speech tracking adapts to complex and realistic listening 581 

environments, refining our understanding of the neural mechanisms underlying spatial 582 

unmasking and SRM. 583 

584 

Conclusion 585 

In summary, the study demonstrates that spatial separation robustly enhances cortical speech 586 

tracking and closely mirrors behavioural SRM. TRFs serve as continuous -speech analog on of 587 

AEPs, capturing hierarchical auditory processing influenced by SNR and spatial configuration. 588 

These findings support neural tracking as a sensitive and objective measure of spatial hearing 589 

and provide a foundation for future work in clinical populations and hearing -device research, 590 

offering both mechanistic insights and practical avenues for assessment and intervention. 591
