# Physics-Informed Direction-of-Arrival Estimation Over Distributed Edge Devices 

Nathan Tatsuta and Rajeev Sahay 

Abstract —Direction-of-arrival (DoA) estimation is a funda-mental array processing task that has benefited substantially from deep learning. Deploying such methods across distributed edge devices introduces privacy and communication constraints that federated learning (FL) can address. Yet, standard FL algorithms treat DoA as a generic classification problem, ignoring the underlying physics of the array manifold. To address this, we propose a physics-informed FL framework for DoA estimation that incorporates steering-vector geometry directly into the local training objective via a manifold-aware regularizer. Unlike existing FL baselines, the regularizer in our framework penalizes discrepancies in steering space rather than label space, exploiting the known geometric structure of the array manifold. We provide theoretical convergence guarantees for our framework, showing convergence to a stationary point. Simulation results confirm that our physics informed approach outperforms multiple FL baseline approaches across iid and non-iid data conditions. 

Index Terms —Array processing, direction of arrival, federated learning, physical-layer communications. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Direction-of-arrival (DoA) estimation is a cornerstone prob-lem in array signal processing, with applications spanning communications, radar, navigation, and localization. Classi-cal methods such as multiple signal classification (MUSIC) and the generalized likelihood ratio test (GLRT) exploit the geometric structure of the array manifold to estimate source directions. Recent deep learning (DL) based approaches have demonstrated comparable performance to such classical meth-ods, particularly under poor channel conditions (e.g., low-SNR and unknown channel state information (CSI) conditions) [1], [2], [3], [4]. The key limitation of DL, however, lies in the need to centralize large datasets from multiple receivers. This training requirement raises privacy and communication con-cerns in distributed sensing systems. Federated learning (FL), a distributed DL training paradigm, addresses this challenge by locally training models on edge devices, keeping raw data local, and only transmitting model weights over the air [5]. Despite its advantage, however, standard FL algorithms such as federated averaging [5], FedProx [6], and SCAFFOLD [7] optimize objectives that minimize generic cross-entropy loss functions, where all angular misclassifications are equally penalized rather than dynamically penalizing close incorrect predictions (e.g., when ˆθ = 6 ◦ and θ = 5 ◦) more than large

Under heterogeneous data distribu-tions, where different arrays observe signals from different angular sectors or different channel conditions, this mismatch compounds, resulting in client models drifting in directions that are geometrically inconsistent with the array manifold and, ultimately, degrading the global model as an accurate angular estimator [8], [9]. Centralized DL has been widely applied to DoA estima-tion, spanning covariance classifiers [1], [2], hybrid data-subspace approaches such as DeepMUSIC [10], augmentations of classical algorithms [3], [4], and raw I/Q convolutional neural networks [11]. These methods assume the centralization of training data, which is impractical in distributed sensing environments. In such distributed environments, FL has been applied to related array processing tasks such as channel estimation in multiple-input-multiple-output (MIMO) systems

IV. E XPERIMENTAL SETUP AND RESULTS 

A. Experimental Setup 

We generated 180,000 signals in MATLAB, each with N =1500 snapshots using M = 8 array elements. Each signal, 

Z = [ Z1, Z2] ∈ CM ×N , is comprised of Z1 ∈ CM ×N/ 2,which contains only noise, and Z2 ∈ CM ×N/ 2, which contains both the signal of interest and noise. We concatenate the two covariance matrices and separate the real and imaginary components to obtain input X = [ R1, R2] ∈ RM ×2M ×2. The angle of arrival was restricted to [−60 ◦, 60 ◦] in 2◦ increments, for 61 candidate angles. We evaluated four experimental conditions: (1) label-iid/channel-iid, (2) label-iid/channel-non-iid, (3) label-non-iid/channel-iid, and (4) label-non-iid/channel-non-iid. The number of clients was K ∈ {6, 12 }. These conditions represented varying levels of angular and channel condi-tion heterogeneity. We modeled channel fading using Gaus-sian, Rayleigh, and Rician distributions. Both FedDoA and DoAProx are architecture-agnostic; for our experiments we instantiate g(w; ·) as the CNN of [15], reproduced in Table II. We compare FedDoA and DoAProx to three non-physics-informed state-of-the-art FL baselines: FedAvg [5], Fed-Prox [6], and SCAFFOLD [7]. In all simulations, we used 

T = 200 and minibatch sizes of 128. SCAFFOLD used learning rate 3 · 10 −4 for label-non-iid conditions and 1 · 10 −4

otherwise; all other algorithms used 1·10 −3. Physics-informed weights were β = 10 −3 and μ = 10 . All hyperparameters were selected via hyperparameter search per algorithm.

V. C ONCLUSION 

We introduced FedDoA and DoAProx, the first physics-informed federated learning algorithms for DoA estimation, which embed steering-vector geometry directly into the lo-cal training objective of federated learning. We theoretically 5

proved convergence to a stationary point for both methods and derived the first direct MSAE bound for federated DoA learning. Empirically, our physics-informed framework outper-formed FedAvg, FedProx, and SCAFFOLD across iid and non-iid conditions, closely approaching centralized performance.
