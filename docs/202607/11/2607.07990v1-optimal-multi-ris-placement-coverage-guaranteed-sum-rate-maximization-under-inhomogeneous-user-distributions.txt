# Optimal Multi-RIS Placement: Coverage-Guaranteed Sum Rate Maximization Under Inhomogeneous User Distributions 

Abhishek Rajasekaran , Member, IEEE , Mehdi Karbalayghareh , Member, IEEE ,Xiaoyan Ma , Member, IEEE , David J. Love , Fellow, IEEE ,and Christopher G. Brinton , Senior Member, IEEE 

Abstract —The realization of the full potential of Reconfig-urable Intelligent Surfaces (RIS) in a wireless system is tied to their strategic spatial deployment. While existing literature primarily focuses on enabling fairness by maximizing coverage to navigate through obstacles, these approaches often fail to exploit the spatial distribution of user density to maximize throughput. Thus, to enable fairness without loss in throughput, we formulate a novel hierarchical problem that maximizes the expected sum rate of the system while guaranteeing probabilistic coverage with the least possible number of RISs deployed. To solve this multi-layered non-convex problem, firstly, we obtain optimal regions where we can deploy RISs to provide the coverage guarantee by solving a constrained set-cover problem on a visibility graph. Then, the minimum number of RISs we require to satisfy the coverage guarantee is obtained by a greedy minimum partitioning on an intersection hypergraph formed using the optimal regions. Finally, a Bayesian Optimization based approach is used to compute the final optimal RIS placement. Numerical results are provided to show that the proposed framework consistently identifies placements that jointly achieve good coverage and throughput, without impractical system assumptions. 

Index Terms —Reconfigurable Intelligent Surfaces (RIS), op-timal positioning, coverage, set cover, intersection hypergraphs, minimum partitioning, Bayesian optimization. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# THE ITU-R Recommendation M.2160-0 envisions 6G (IMT-2030) to further advance enhanced Mobile Broad-band (eMBB) and move towards immersive communication with a massive increase in data rates [2]. Reconfigurable Intelligent Surface (RIS) is typically a planar surface with numerous passive reflecting elements made of a metamate-rial [3], [4] and connected to a smart controller. The nature of the metamaterial is such that these elements can help alter the amplitude and/or phase shifts of the reflected signal in an intelligent manner [5], [6], due to which RIS has convincingly emerged as a tool to improve the throughput of the system [7], [8]. Another role that an RIS can play in a network is to en-hance the coverage of a base station (BS) in wireless network by overcoming blockages created by obstacles [9], [10]. A. Related Works 

Many existing works that discuss RIS placement use cov-erage maximization approaches listed in [15], [16]. These works propose to use geometric approaches to help the desired signals navigate around obstacles by positioning RIS(s) at relevant positions. But they do not have the capability to choose a location that maximizes the sum rate of the system. On the other hand, [17] presents an algorithm that maximizes sum rate by jointly optimizing RIS placement, CSI error, transmit beamformers, and RIS phase coefficients. This real-time optimization of RIS placement (means RIS placement should change in real-time) using a simplified closed form solution works only for fixed number of users and fixed user locations. A similar method – another closed form solution for optimal placement using an outage probability approach (followed by a series of simplifications) is discussed in [18]. It is shown later that we can do better than such purely-distance dependent closed form solutions for RIS placement. Certain works including [19]–[25] have also discussed op-timization of RIS placement for specific applications like Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAV) based communication, high-speed train communication, VLC (Visual Light Communica-tion) and communication between autonomous vehicles and for spectrum co-existence with radars. Because the objectives of these approaches are tailored to specific requirements of each application, none of them offer generality of the proposed method, which can be applied universally. arXiv:2607.07990v1 [eess.SP] 8 Jul 2026 2

The other important problem, throughput maximization, is typically dealt with by jointly optimizing the transmit beamformer and RIS coefficients in real-time. Let us define this problem as the joint beamforming problem. By definition, to perform joint beamforming for the current ‘channel state’, we need to obtain the corresponding channel state information (CSI). To address this, [26]–[29] present various channel es-timation approaches for RIS-assisted systems. These methods can be extended for the multi-RIS case by sequentially turning off and on each of the RISs. A recent work has been proposed to use Orthogonal Complement Projection to reduce this overhead [30]. Since the proposed RIS placement algorithm remains effective for any channel estimation technique, it provides room to assume these or even future approaches. With estimated CSI for the RIS-assisted system, we can perform joint beamforming. [31] presents a well-accepted fractional programming approach to maximize the weighted sum rate of the system for a single RIS-assisted wireless system. As an extension, [17], [32] present a similar fractional programming based beamforming for a multi-RIS assisted system. [33] presents another similar approach while also con-sidering hardware impairments and channel uncertainties. On the other hand, [34] presents a hybrid beamforming approach to maximize the sum rate of an RIS-assisted system. Some recent works [35], [36] even use Machine Leaning. In summary, based on the survey, we see that although the RIS-assisted system is well-studied, there is a practically rele-vant problem that remains untouched. The existing coverage-based RIS placement approaches ensure fairness by reaching all users, but aren’t capable of maximizing throughput (sum rate) and the sum rate maximization approaches requires RIS location to change in real-time which isn’t practical. Therefore, there is a clear scope to propose an RIS placement technique that does both – to maximize expected system throughput while ensuring a level of fairness, making use of minimum resources. B. Contributions 

Motivated by the research gap, we propose a novel scheme to maximize the expected sum rate of the system with a prob-abilistic coverage guarantee, while using minimum number of RISs. The key contributions include:  

> •

Firstly, a detailed justification is provided to show that a simple coverage maximization or a sum rate maximiza-tion is an oversimplification. Then, a unique hierarchical softly-coupled formulation is proposed to better deal with the placement problem. More specifically, we propose to first compute the minimum number of RISs and a search space to provide a probabilistic coverage guarantee. Then, we propose to optimize RIS placement by maximizing the expected sum rate performance on this space. This formulation, to the best of our knowledge, is the first attempt to simultaneously consider expected throughput, probabilistic coverage and number of RISs.  

> •

To solve for the search space where RISs can be placed to satisfy the probabilistic coverage requirement, firstly, we propose and prove a lemma to reduce this global prob-abilistic coverage guarantee to simpler local conditions that can be applied to the regions shadowed by each of the obstacles. To reduce the number of computations, instead of handling each obstacle and the areas they shadow individually, we propose to form clusters among obstacles. Then, a theorem is proposed and proved to define candidate sets for RIS placement that satisfy the derived local coverage requirements. Finally, we propose to solve a constrained set-cover problem on a visibility graph to calculate these candidate sets for placement.  

> •

Then, to solve for the minimum number of RISs we require to satisfy the coverage requirement, we construct an intersection hypergraph [37] using these candidate sets. Taking inspiration from [38], we propose a greedy maximal hypergraph partitioning algorithm to reduce the number of candidate sets to the lowest possible. Thus, we get the minimum number of RISs and also compute the corresponding reduced search space for RIS placement.  

> •

Finally, we propose a novel technique that combines user distribution-driven learning and conventional joint beamforming techniques to calculate the optimal RIS placement. Assuming that the deployed system would use a joint beamforming strategy after the deployment of the RIS-assisted system, we use a two-dimensional Bayesian Optimization (2D-BO) based approach to maximize the expected sum rate over the user distribution [39]. [1] was our preliminary paper that introduced this idea. In this work, we address its limitations with a more general formulation for multiple RISs that provides explicit control over the throughput-coverage tradeoff and incorporates both resource minimization and search space determination (to re-duce computational cost). Furthermore, we use a more efficient 2D-BO for throughput maximization, which converges faster.

Firstly, a technique is proposed to compute the search space spanned by constraints from Eqs. (8a) and (8b). Then, an elegant method is proposed to solve the subproblem, P2 .1.

A. Preliminary Processing 1) Discretization of the Space: To optimize the placement of the RISs, we first discretize the continuous cell O into S ⊂ 

R2 representing a finite grid of points S = {s1, s2, . . . , sL}. A subset of these points correspond to the obstacle configuration. Let us denote this set as Q ⊂ S . And let us also define sb ∈ S 

to be the discretized version of ob defined earlier. Let us define ‘visible set’ as the set of vertices that have a direct LoS to the BS, V = {s ∈ S \ Q | L(sb, s) = 1 }.Finally, let us also define S∗ = {s∗ ∈ S| λu(s∗)̸ = 0 } ⊂ S 

to be a subset of S which have a non-zero probability of user existence as per the defined user distribution fu. This is the set of points we need to extend coverage from the BS. Further, let us also define S′ = S \ S ∗ which are those points which have a zero probability of user existence. 

2) Obstacle Clusters: Then, instead of handling I obstacles and the areas they shadow individually, we form T ≪ I

clusters among the discretized obstacle set, Q. This allows us to treat each of these clusters as “large obstacles” and considerably reduces the number of computations for the over-all solution. To form physically meaningful clusters among these obstacle points, we adopt the Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (DBSCAN) algorithm [40]. DBSCAN is particularly effective here as it does not require the number of clusters to be pre-defined and can identify clusters among a variety of shapes. In this algorithm, each obstacle cluster Qi, ∀1 ≤ i ≤ T is identified based on a neighborhood radius parameter and a lower bound on the number of points in a cluster. This procedure yields dis-joint obstacle clusters {Q 1, Q2, . . . , QT } such that Qi ⊆ Q ,

Qi ∩ O j = ∅ for all i̸ = j, 1 ≤ i, j ≤ T , and STi=1 Qi = Q .

Having established the superior performance of the pro-posed scheme across baselines, we want to further investigate the choice of an important parameter, the coverage threshold, 

pcth . In Fig. 6b, we observe that as we increase this threshold from 0% to 100% , the search space for RIS placement reduces from the set of all reachable points from the BS to a much smaller set. In Fig. 6a, we see that increasing the threshold leads to a clear increase in the coverage of the system. On the other hand, we also observe that the sum rate performance also increases considerably till the threshold reaches a 50%

This observation aligns perfectly with the inferences and remarks made in Section VI-E. Thus, the suggested choice of pcth would be 50% to 75% , unless a specific application requires it to be more one-sided. VII. C ONCLUSION 

A novel, practically relevant problem formulation and an elegant solution were proposed. The proposed work tackled typical practical requirements of an RIS-assisted system, such as exploiting the user distribution to maximize performance, exploiting the obstacle configuration to maximize the coverage

This is the first work, to the best of our knowledge, to handle the ideal hierarchical optimization directly - maximizing coverage, while also ensuring good throughput. Also, in the proposed solution, a new and powerful approach that combined prior-driven learning with conventional beamforming to maximize expected post-deployment throughput was introduced. Results indicated an improvement in sum rate performance when there was a considerable increase in the coverage of the system, but this trend reversed beyond a certain coverage level. This behavior underscored a fundamental tradeoff in the design of an obstacle-deterred RIS-assisted system: a purely coverage-driven placement strategy narrows the search to regions that achieve near-complete coverage, but at the cost of placing RISs in locations that are suboptimal for signal enhance-ment, degrading sum rate performance. Conversely, a purely performance-driven strategy risks placing RISs at locations that maximize sum rate for only those users it can reach already. The proposed algorithm navigated this tradeoff by jointly accounting for both objectives, arriving at a placement that achieved strong coverage without sacrificing in sum rate gains. This balance between coverage and performance is what practical RIS deployment demands, and results validate that the proposed framework reliably achieves this balance across a variety of obstacle configurations and user distributions. In future works, we will expand the idea for RIS-assisted ISAC systems to place RIS to maximize communication throughput and sensing accuracy. 12
