ABSTRACT This paper introduces the topology-independent distributed multichannel Wiener filter (TI-dMWF), a novel algorithm for distributed node-specific signal estimation in wireless acoustic sensor networks (WASNs) with unconstrained topologies. The TI-dMWF enables each node in the network to compute its centralized multichannel Wiener filter solution by exchanging only low-dimensional fused signals, without requiring iterative estimation, unlike state-of-the-art approaches such as the topology-independent distributed adaptive node-specific signal estimation (TI-DANSE) algorithm. The TI-dMWF is proven optimal when each source is observed by either all nodes or only one node. Theoretical analysis and numerical simulations confirm that it achieves centralized estimation performance in a single run. Its latency as a function of the pruned-tree depth and its computational complexity are also analyzed. Its robustness is assessed in reverberant-room simulations under estimated second-order statistics, various network topologies, and deviations from the assumed observability model.

I. Introduction 

# IN recent years, devices capable of recording, process-ing, and exchanging audio data wirelessly have become ubiquitous, enabling the creation of wireless acoustic sensor networks (WASNs), which have gained attention for applica-tions such as environmental monitoring and smart homes [1], [2]. Within a WASN, devices (nodes) equipped with one or more microphones (sensors), such as smartphones, laptops, or hearing aids, collaborate to perform audio signal processing tasks, e.g., noise reduction [3]–[5], dereverberation [6], [7], or sound source localization [8], [9]. Compared to traditional fixed microphone arrays, WASNs offer greater spatial coverage, deployment flexibility, and robustness to node failures [1], but operate under constraints such as communication cost and potentially dynamic topologies, making the design of distributed algorithms that respect these constraints a key challenge. This paper addresses node-specific signal estimation in a WASN, where each node estimates a signal of interest (e.g., speech) from its own sensor signals and signals sent by other nodes. The optimal centralized linear minimum mean square error (LMMSE) solution to this problem is provided as the multichannel Wiener filter (MWF) [10, Ch.2], but computing the MWF directly requires access to all sensor signals in the network, which is often infeasible due to communication cost constraints and in WASNs that are not

The distributed multichannel Wiener filter (dMWF) [11] lets each node of a FC WASN solve its node-specific LMMSE problem distributively by exchanging only dimensionally reduced (fused) signals, addressing the communication cost constraint. However, topologies may not be FC and may vary over time. We here propose the topology-independent distributed multichannel Wiener filter (TI-dMWF), which lets nodes of a topology-unconstrained WASN compute the centralized MWF as if they had access to all network signals, while exchanging only fused signals. Here, topology-unconstrained is a property of the WASN and topology-independent (TI) is a property of an algorithm: the TI-dMWF is a topology-independent algorithm over topology-unconstrained WASNs. As the dMWF, it is itera-tionless, unlike the topology-independent distributed adaptive node-specific signal estimation (TI-DANSE) [12] and TI-DANSE + [13] algorithms. The TI-DANSE algorithm belongs to the distributed adaptive signal fusion (DASF) framework [14], which the distributed adaptive node-specific signal fusion (DANSF) framework [15] extends to node-specific problems. Note that TI-DANSE + is closely related to DANSF [13]. Unlike all these iterative algorithms, the TI-dMWF reaches the centralized MWF in a single discovery-estimation pass in global-local subspaces (GLS) scenarios (defined hereafter). Unlike the dMWF, which handles any partially overlapping desired subspaces (PODS) scenario [11], the TI-dMWF targets GLS scenarios, where each (speech or noise) source is observed by either all nodes (a global source) or only one node (a local source). Such scenarios arise, e.g., in airports or train stations: a global source is a signal propagating throughout the environment and picked up by all nodes (e.g., a public-address or alarm announcement), while a local source is observed by a single node (e.g., a nearby talker or localized noise). The more general case where a source is observed by an arbitrary subset of nodes is described by the PODS model; the GLS model considered here remains a relevant special case whenever network-wide and strictly local sources are clearly separated. The GLS assumption differs from the fully overlapping desired subspaces (FODS) assumption [11] (all speech sources observed by all nodes) needed for TI-DANSE and TI-DANSE + convergence and optimality [12], [13].

> V. Latency and Complexity Analysis

Real-time feasibility depends on the per-frame latency Lk,dominated in the TI-dMWF by the downstream data flow at every frame. Let δhop be the average per-hop delay (transmission and per-node time to compute (4)). Since each node q must gather its upstream neighbors’ fused signals before transmitting its own fused signals, the per-frame latency for root k is Lk = ∆ kδhop , with ∆k the pruned-tree depth. Topology-independence lets one choose a pruning strategy minimizing this depth: whereas an MST minimizes total communication power but may yield deeper trees, an SPT rooted at k minimizes ∆k [25]. The real-time constraint Lk <Tshift (with Tshift the STFT frame shift) requires δhop <Tshift /∆k; for Tshift = 20 ms and ∆k = 4 , this allows 

δhop up to 5 ms, which is attainable by low-latency wireless protocols. A separate discovery latency is associated with the up-stream signal flooding. Since the discovery step runs only every Nus frames, independently of the per-frame estimation path, the upstream statistics may be delivered asynchronously or with delay without affecting optimality in slowly varying environments. Moreover, once the SCMs are well-estimated, the TI-dMWF reaches centralized performance in a single discovery cycle. The per-frame computational complexity of the TI-dMWF stays low by decoupling the frequent downstream fusion from the infrequent upstream discovery. > VI. Numerical Simulations

This section reports four experiments. The first (Section VI-A )validates the framework using oracle SCMs in idealized scenarios. The second (Section VI-B) also uses oracle SCMs, isolating model mismatch from estimation errors to assess robustness to deviations from the GLS assumption. The third and fourth (Section VI-C ) evaluate the TI-dMWF in simulated reverberant scenarios with SCMs estimated from the microphone signals, assessing respectively the convergence under estimated SCMs and the influence of the tree-pruning strategy. 

> A. Validation under Oracle SCMs

As a first experiment, the framework is validated using oracle SCMs. The TI-dMWF is compared in an ideal setting to TI-DANSE [12] (with the filter-normalization of [26]) and TI-DANSE + [13], validating the TI-dMWF framework and assessing the convergence of the iterative baselines. Since TI-DANSE + is closely related to DANSF [13], [15], this also indirectly contrasts the TI-dMWF with the node-specific extension of the DASF framework. > B. Robustness to GLS Deviations

The second experiment assesses TI-dMWF’s sensitivity to deviations from the GLS assumption via a leakage parameter 

α ∈ [0 , 1] . Sources unobserved under the assumed GLS pattern leak into the corresponding microphone signals with gain α, so α = 0 is the strict GLS scenario and increasing α

injects each source’s energy into the nodes the pattern treats as non-observing. The algorithm keeps operating under the assumed pattern as the actual signals depart from it. To isolate model mismatch, it uses oracle SCMs and the idealized setup of Section VI-A with random steering matrices. Figure 1b shows the TI-dMWF MSE W and MSE d versus 

α, with the centralized, local, and unprocessed references.

At α = 0 (strict GLS), the TI-dMWF attains the centralized MWF exactly, its network-wide filter coinciding with the centralized MWF to machine precision ( MSE W ≈ 10 −16 )and its estimate reaching the centralized MSE d, as predicted by Theorem 1. Any non-zero leakage immediately breaks this exact optimality, with no finite tolerance band: MSE W

leaves machine precision and rises continuously. This matches Remark 1 of Section III-D: once a source is observed by a strict subset of nodes, the non-global components { ˙yq }q∈K

in (18) is lost, and the fused signals are no longer a sufficient statistic. Exact optimality is confined to the strict GLS scenario, but away from it the TI-dMWF degrades smoothly and monotonically with α. Nonetheless, across the whole 

α range the TI-dMWF network-wide filter stays closer to the centralized MWF than the local MWF, and its estimate stays more accurate than the local MWF and the unprocessed input. 

> C. Performance under Estimated SCMs

Beyond the idealized setting of Section VI-A , the TI-dMWF is evaluated in reverberant scenarios with all SCMs estimated from the microphone signals. room impulse responses (RIRs) are generated with the image-source method (ISM) in Pyroomacoustics [28] for a 5×5×3 m room with T60 = 0 .2 s. As in Section VI-A , the WASN has K = 6 nodes with 

Mk = 5 microphones (compact 3 cm-radius arrays), placed at least 0.25 m from the walls, with a C = 0 .5 ad-hoc topology pruned via the SPT strategy unless stated otherwise. The scene contains Q(s) = 2 speech sources (VCTK [29]) and 

Q(n) = 2 babble noise sources, at least 1 m from any node, at 0 dB mean signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) (latent speech vs. noise), with sensor self-noise 20 dB below the source power. The cGLS observability follows Section VI-A : one noise source is observed by all nodes, one by a single node. Microphone signals are obtained by time-domain con-volution of the latent source signals with the RIRs and processed in a weighted overlap-add (WOLA) filter bank (1024 -sample discrete Fourier transform (DFT), 50 % overlap, Hann window). Speech sources follow a 3 s on-off cycle with randomized start times. The SCMs are estimated by VAD-based recursive averaging (3) with forgetting factor β,from a random initialization; an imperfect VAD flips each per-source frame decision with probability pe. Each 40 sscenario is averaged over 20 runs. Under estimated SCMs, a rank- ◦

Fusion matrices and filters are recomputed every Nus = 5 frames ( 160 ms, cf. Sec-tion V) while the SCMs update every frame. Performance is assessed by means of the short-term objective intelligibility (STOI) [31] over sliding windows, averaged over all K nodes. > VII. Conclusion

We have introduced the TI-dMWF, an algorithm for dis-tributed signal estimation in topology-unconstrained WASNs  

> VOLUME , 7P. Didier et al.: Distributed Multichannel Wiener Filtering for Topology-Unconstrained Wireless Acoustic Sensor Networks:

that lets each node compute its node-specific centralized MWF by exchanging only low-dimensional fused signals, without iterative filter estimation, unlike state-of-the-art algorithms. Simulations confirm that the TI-dMWF is optimal in GLS scenarios, where each source is observed by all nodes or only one. Latency and complexity analyses show that the TI-dMWF retains centralized optimality in the GLS setting with predictable latency, computational load, and communication cost, and degrades controllably when the GLS assumption only approximately holds. Future work may relax the GLS assumption and validate the algorithm in real-world scenarios beyond the present simulation study, and generalize the upstream signal construction (7) beyond the selection-matrix formulation to handle ◦
