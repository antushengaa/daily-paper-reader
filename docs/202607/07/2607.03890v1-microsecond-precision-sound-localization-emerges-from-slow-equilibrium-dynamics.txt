Precise sound localization relies on microsecond sensitivity to interaural time differences (ITDs), yet binaural perception exhibits sluggish tracking of dynamic acoustic cues. How these properties coexist remains unresolved. Here, ITD is rep-resented as a stable equilibrium of neural population dynamics rather than by the classical place-coding framework originally proposed by Jeffress in 1948. In this framework, excitatory and inhibitory interactions across frequency channels generate a population signal that drives a dynamical system toward an equi-librium corresponding to the estimated ITD. Despite relying on relatively slow temporal dynamics, the model achieves microsecond-level precision and repro-duces key physiological observations, including frequency-dependent best-delay distributions, without requiring explicit delay lines or precisely timed inhibition. These findings provide a potential explanation for how precise ITD sensitivity can arise from slow neural dynamics. 

Accurate sound localization is a fundamental capability of biological auditory systems. For low-frequency sounds, localization in the horizontal plane relies primarily on ITDs, which typically range from several tens to a few hundreds of microseconds depending on head size. Remarkably, the temporal resolution of ITD sensitivity can reach the order of 10 𝜇 s ( 1,2 ). Since the seminal work of 1

Jeffress ( 3), neural processing of ITDs has classically been explained by a place-code architecture based on axonal delay lines and coincidence detectors as illustrated in Fig.1A. Different neurons are maximally activated by different ITD values, thereby forming a spatial map of sound azimuth. However, while systematic delay lines and spatial maps of ITD have been convincingly demonstrated in avian species ( 4, 5 ), their applicability to mammals has remained under discussion (6–8 ). In mammals, anatomical evidence for precisely arranged delay lines is limited, and physiological recordings reveal ITD tuning characteristics that are not readily explained by a straightforward place code ( 9, 10 ).A notable observation is that ITD-sensitive neurons in the mammalian medial superior olive (MSO) often exhibit best delays (BDs) extending beyond the ecologically relevant ITD range imposed by head size ( 6, 11 ). These findings are not readily reconciled with a simple place-based representation of ITD based on single-neuron tuning peaks. Consistent with this view, physiological studies have demonstrated that precisely timed inhibition plays a critical role in shaping ITD sensitivity in the mammalian MSO ( 12 ). Rather than being determined solely by axonal delay lines, ITD tuning has been proposed to arise from the interaction between excitatory and temporally precise inhibitory inputs. However, this interpretation has been challenged, particularly with respect to whether inhibition can provide the temporal precision required for microsecond ITD tuning ( 10 ). As a more fundamental issue, it remains unclear how the auditory system reconciles its re-markable microsecond sensitivity to ITDs with its limited ability to track rapidly changing ITDs, a phenomenon known as binaural sluggishness ( 13–15 ). Psychophysical studies have shown that the binaural system is primarily sensitive to temporal modulations on the order of 50–200 ms, indicating a substantial limitation in tracking dynamic changes in interaural cues. Although a more recent study demonstrated that faster modulations can be detected when the stimulus is carefully designed ( 16 ), the temporal resolution remains, at best, on the order of milliseconds – still far from the microsecond precision observed in static ITD sensitivity. In contrast, the Jeffress model, based on coincidence detection, predicts instantaneous responses to ITD changes. This apparent paradox suggests that the mechanisms underlying ITD sensitivity and temporal tracking are not yet fully understood, pointing to a fundamental gap in our understanding of binaural temporal processing. In this study, ITD is proposed to be encoded as a stable equilibrium of a dynamical system driven by neural population activity, as illustrated in Fig. 1B. Within this framework, ITD sensi-2tivity on the order of 10 𝜇 s emerges naturally, despite neural dynamics operating on time scales of tens of milliseconds. Importantly, the proposed model does not rely on conventional place coding mechanisms, nor does it require precisely timed inhibitory interactions. In the following, simula-tion results are presented to demonstrate the model’s capability to account for key experimental observations, and the theoretical formulation of the model is described. 

# ITD estimation through equilibrium dynamics 

The main structure of the proposed model is outlined here, while the detailed formulation is provided in the Supplementary Materials. Sounds entering the left and right ears are decomposed into frequency components in the cochlea, and the resulting signals are projected to populations of units in the MSO for each frequency channel. The schematic shown in Fig. 1B illustrates the neural circuit corresponding to a single frequency channel receiving inputs from both ears. From the excitatory inputs of the two ears, a running interaural cross-correlation function is computed in excitatory units (red dashed traces, Eq. S1). An inhibitory unit (black dashed traces) with a slightly different BD is then introduced. Their interaction produces an excitatory–inhibitory (E–I) response (blue solid traces, Eq. S2). The responses of all E–I units are summed across units and frequency channels and normalized to yield a population response 𝐺 (𝑡 ) (Eq. S7), shown as the blue curve in the bottom panel of Fig. 1B. This population response serves as the driving input to the dynamical system governing ITD estimation:

These results indicate that precise ITD estimation can be achieved through slow convergence dynamics, as illustrated in Fig. 2A. This property provides a potential resolution to the apparent paradox between microsecond-level ITD sensitivity and limited temporal resolution in dynamic binaural processing. 

# Single neuron response 

The proposed model is shown to be consistent with physiological observations. Fig. 3A shows the ITD tuning function of a typical MSO neuron with a best frequency of approximately 1 kHz, reported by Brand et al. ( 12 ). The blue curve represents the tuning function measured under normal conditions, exhibiting a BD of approximately 170 𝜇 s. This value exceeds the ecological range for the gerbil, which is about 120 𝜇 s. In contrast, when inhibition is pharmacologically blocked by applying the glycine antagonist strychnine, the tuning curve (red line) shifts, resulting in a BD of approximately 50 𝜇 s, which falls within the ecological range. These results indicate that inhibition plays a crucial role in shaping ITD tuning and can shift the neuron’s preferred delay beyond the range predicted by simple anatomical delays. The proposed model reproduces this behavior, as shown in Fig. 3B. The neural response is assumed to arise from the interaction between excitatory and inhibitory inputs (blue curve; 𝑟 EI 𝑖 𝑗 in Eq. S2). In the absence of inhibition, the response corresponds to the purely excitatory component (red curve; 𝑟 EE 𝑖 𝑗 in Eq. S1), whose BD is set to 50 𝜇 s. An inhibitory unit with a symmetric BD of 

−50 𝜇 s (black curve; 𝑟 EE 𝑖𝑘 in Eq. S1) is further assumed. The interaction between the excitatory 4and inhibitory components effectively shifts the peak of the response function, yielding an E–I response with a BD of approximately 180 𝜇 s, consistent with the physiological observations shown in Fig. 3A ( 12 ). This indicates that large apparent BDs can emerge even when the individual component BDs lie within the ecological range. Because inhibitory inputs of this type are difficult to isolate in physiological experiments, the role of such mechanisms may have remained largely overlooked in previous studies. 

# Population and frequency dependence 

Multiple units, as described above, are assumed to exist within each auditory filter channel, char-acterized by its peak frequency 𝑓 𝑝𝑖 . To construct the model, a minimal constraint is imposed: the BDs of excitatory units are positive, whereas those of inhibitory units are negative. Within each fre-quency channel, the range of BDs is limited to ±50 · 1000 / 𝑓 𝑝𝑖 (𝜇 s), corresponding to one-twentieth of the period of a sinusoid at the peak frequency 𝑓 𝑝𝑖 . The BDs are randomly assigned within this range from a uniform distribution. For each frequency channel, ten E–I units are assumed. Fig. 4A illustrates this configuration. Through combinations of excitatory and inhibitory units, the resulting E–I units exhibit BDs that extend beyond the ecological range of ±120 𝜇 s. Notably, even when both the excitatory and inhibitory unit BDs fall within the ecological range ( △, ▽), the resulting E–I unit BD ( ◦) can lie well outside this range. When this condition is not satisfied ( + and 

×), the resulting E–I unit BD ( ∗) can become even larger. Fig. 4B shows the ITD tuning functions of E–I units corresponding to the colored filled circles in Fig. 4A. These results are consistent with physiological observations reported in previous studies ( 6, 10, 17 ). The equilibrium dynamics shown in Fig. 2 arise from the integration of activity across these populations. Taken together, these findings indicate that the distribution of BDs in E–I units can naturally span a wide range of ITDs and exhibit frequency dependence. Importantly, the mapping between excitatory and inhibitory unit BDs does not require precise pairing; as long as a simple sign constraint is satisfied, random combinations are sufficient. Consequently, ITD estimation can be achieved without precise tuning at the level of individual units. This provides a simple and robust explanation for the broad distribution of BDs observed in physiological studies, without requiring the precisely tuned delays assumed in place-coding models. 5Discussion 

The classical Jeffress model explains ITD coding solely through excitatory coincidence detection and does not require inhibition. However, the presence of inhibitory inputs in the MSO has been well established ( 11 ), leading to extensions of the Jeffress framework that incorporate inhibition ( 18,19 ). Despite these modifications, the fundamental coding principle in these models remains based on place coding, in which ITD is represented by patterns of activity across neurons tuned to different delays. In contrast, the proposed model relies essentially on inhibition and represents ITD through population coding governed by equilibrium dynamics. Within this framework, binaural sluggishness arises naturally from the slow convergence of the underlying dynamics. Thus, whereas the Jeffress model implies instantaneous localization, the proposed model provides a mechanism through which slow neural dynamics can coexist with microsecond-level ITD sensitivity. Joris and Yin ( 10 ) have raised several important issues regarding existing mechanisms of ITD estimation. First, they questioned the hypothesis proposed by Brand et al. ( 12 ) that precisely timed inhibition is required for ITD coding. In contrast, the present model does not require dynamically generated precise timing. Instead, it assumes only a slight temporal delay of inhibitory inputs relative to excitatory inputs, together with fixed but randomly distributed BDs. Consequently, microsecond-level temporal precision need not be realized at the level of individual units. Second, the Jeffress model assumes that BDs lie within the ecological range and are largely independent of best frequency. However, physiological observations in mammals reveal substantial frequency dependence in BD distributions, with broader distributions at lower frequencies ( 6, 10, 17 ). The proposed model naturally reproduces this characteristic through excitatory–inhibitory interactions (Fig. 4), suggesting that large apparent BDs need not be directly encoded at the level of individual units but may instead emerge from combinations of excitatory and inhibitory units. Third, the present framework allows accurate ITD estimation to be achieved within a unilateral neural circuit, without requiring explicit comparison across hemispheres. This is consistent with observations noted in ( 10 ), suggesting limited physiological support for hemispheric integration. Although incorporating bilateral information may further improve estimation accuracy, it is not essential for the core computation. 6Finally, Joris and Yin ( 10 ) emphasized that binaural processing is important not only for ITD estimation but also for representing interaural correlation in complex acoustic environments. They further noted that neurons with tuning functions resembling the blue curve in Fig. 3B would be expected to show little sensitivity to changes in interaural correlation ( 𝜌 ) near ITD = 0. In the Supplementary Material (Sensitivity to interaural correlation), model responses were evaluated as a function of 𝜌 , revealing systematic changes in both the mean and variance of the response. Taken together, these results indicate that the proposed model addresses, at least qualitatively, the major issues raised by Joris and Yin ( 10 ). The present model computes ITD through dynamic convergence to an equilibrium point. Similar computational principles have been identified in other neural systems, such as eye-position control (20 ) and head-direction representation ( 21, 22 ). In these systems, variables are represented not by place coding but as stable states of neural dynamics. Importantly, these functions and ITD-based sound localization share a common computational objective: estimating behaviorally relevant spatial variables that may be critical for survival, such as the locations of prey or predators. Modeling the neural response 

The peripheral auditory processing up to the MSO follows conventional extension models of Jeffress-type frameworks ( 18, 19, 23 ). For cochlear frequency analysis, the linear passive gam-machirp (GC) filterbank ( 24 ) was adopted. The peak frequencies of the filters range from 100 Hz to 1.5 kHz, consistent with bandwidth characteristics reported for guinea pigs ( 25 ), cats ( 26 ), and gerbils ( 27 ). The sampling rate was set to 𝑓 𝑠 = 96 kHz, corresponding to a temporal resolution of 10.4 𝜇 s. The output of the filterbank is processed by the Meddis hair-cell model ( 28 ), producing a response representing the probability distribution of auditory nerve firing. Although bushy cells in the anteroventral cochlear nucleus (AVCN) are known to enhance phase locking ( 9), this stage is omitted here, as it does not critically affect the behavior examined in this study. As discussed in the main text, Joris and Yin ( 10 ) argued that a plausible model of ITD processing should account not only for ITD estimation itself but also for binaural sensitivity to interaural correlation ( 𝜌 ). In fact, Gabriel and Colburn ( 29 ) reported remarkably high human sensitivity to changes in interaural correlation. For narrowband noise, the just noticeable difference (JND) in interaural correlation ( Δ𝜌 ) was found to be smaller than 0.01 and to increase with stimulus bandwidth. Any biologically plausible model of binaural processing should therefore be capable of supporting such sensitivity. To examine this issue, the response of the proposed model was evaluated as a function of 𝜌 .The value of 𝜌 was varied from +1 to −1 in steps of 0.05. The stimulus consisted of bandpass noise spanning 100–1000 Hz, generated by summing sinusoidal components at 1-Hz intervals with random initial phases. For each value of 𝜌 , 30 equilibrium values were obtained from independently generated noise realizations. The results are shown in Fig. S1. The variance of the estimated ITD was small for 𝜌 = ±1and substantially larger near 𝜌 = 0. In general, the variance increased as the absolute value of 𝜌 

decreased. This behavior is consistent with the reduced ability to localize sound sources when the binaural signals become less correlated. The observed dependence of response variability on 𝜌 may therefore be related to the perceptual distinction between a sharply localized sound source and a diffuse auditory image. A one-way ANOVA revealed a significant effect of 𝜌 (𝐹 (40 , 1189 ) = 89 .30, 

𝑝 ≪ 0.001). Tukey’s HSD multiple-comparison test ( 𝛼 = 0.05) was subsequently performed. In Fig. S1, only comparisons that differed significantly from either 𝜌 = 1 or 𝜌 = 0 are shown. Signifi-cant differences were observed for pairs of conditions with modest differences in 𝜌 , demonstrating that the model response is sensitive to changes in interaural correlation. Bartlett’s test rejected the homogeneity-of-variance assumption ( 𝜒 2 (40 ) = 375 .55, 𝑝 ≪ 0.001), indicating significant changes in response variance across 𝜌 values. Taken together, these results show that both the mean and variance of the model response depend on 𝜌 . These results indicate that the proposed model is sensitive to interaural correlation, with both the mean and variance of the response varying systematically as a function of 𝜌 . In this respect, the model is at least qualitatively consistent with the challenge raised by Joris and Yin ( 10 ) that binaural processing mechanisms should account for S5 interaural correlation in addition to ITD estimation. Gabriel and Colburn ( 29 ) reported that, for band-limited noise with an upper frequency near 1 kHz, the JND was approximately Δ𝜌 ≃ 0.02 when the reference sound had 𝜌 = 1, whereas values below Δ𝜌 ≃ 0.03 were observed when referenced to 𝜌 = 0. Although the results in Fig. S1 are qualitatively consistent with these psychophysical observations, the pair of 𝜌 values with the smallest significant difference identified by Tukey’s HSD test does not correspond directly to the reported perceptual thresholds. In addition, JNDs corresponding to 75% correct performance, estimated from Gaussian approximations of the model-output distributions, did not fully account for the psychophysical data. This discrepancy suggests that perceptual sensitivity is not determined solely by the overlap of the model-output distributions. Future work will therefore require an explicit observer model linking the neural representations generated by the present dynamical model to perceptual judgments and testing whether the model can quantitatively account for a broader range of binaural psychophysical findings.
