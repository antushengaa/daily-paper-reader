Modeling echolocation as an active pursuit of information via infotaxis 

Wu-Jung Lee, 1, a) John R. Buck, 2 and Peter L. Tyack 3, b) 1) Applied Physics Laboratory, University of Washington, Seattle, WA 98105, USA

Echolocation is a closed-loop active sensing modality in which animals not only choose how 1

they move to acquire information, but also actively modulate incoming sensory (echo) in-2

formation by shaping the acoustic signals they emit to probe the environment. While many 3

models describe how echolocating animals react to prior echoes by adjusting subsequent 4

behavior, few explicitly model how they cognitively reason about information embedded 5

in echoes when determining future actions. Here, we extend “infotaxis,” an information-6

greedy algorithm originally developed for olfactory search, to sonar sensing by formulating 7

an echolocating agent searching for a single target under sensory uncertainty characterized 8

by probabilities of miss and false alarm. Through analytical and computational analyses, 9

we show that the characteristic exploration-exploitation balance of infotaxis also emerges 10 

in echolocation, and that the efficiency and reliability of infotaxis search depend strongly 11 

on sensory information quality. Compared with a maximum a posteriori agent that always 12 

directs the beam to the most probable target location, the infotaxis agent consistently 13 

completes searches with fewer pings and greater robustness to sensory uncertainty. These 14 

results highlight information as a powerful concept for understanding active sensing and 15 

developing models for sonar-guided autonomy in both biological and engineered systems. 16

I. INTRODUCTION 17 

Echolocation is a sensory modality in which echolocators emit acoustic signals and extract infor-18 

mation from the returning echoes to understand their surroundings. Bats and toothed whales are 19 

two prominent animal groups that have evolved specialized anatomical structures and neurophys-20 

iological mechanisms to employ echolocation as their primary sensing modality to navigate and 21 

forage (Surlykke et al. , 2014). Echolocation is a closed-loop feedback system, where the echoloca-22 

tor adjusts its outgoing signals and movements based on information derived from previous echo 23 

returns under the behavioral goal (Fig. 1A). Echolocation is an “active” sensing modality, both 24 

in the narrow sense that the echolocator actively transmits acoustic energy to interrogate the en-25 

vironment and in the broad sense that the echolocator actively changes the configuration of its 26 

sensing apparatus (e.g. ears), location (e.g., body position with respect to a target), and cognitive 27 

strategies (e.g., attention modulation of the incoming echo-acoustic scene) to acquire information 28 

(Moss et al. , 2023; Zweifel and Hartmann, 2020). 29 

Decades of research in both laboratory and field settings have revealed how bats and toothed 30 

whales adapt echolocation behaviors to better solve problems as the task and environment change 31 

(Moss et al. , 2023). For example, bats modulate pulse duration and inter-pulse intervals according 32 

to their attended range and clutter around the target (e.g., Aytekin et al. , 2010; Falk et al. ,33 

2014; Wilkinson et al. , 2025), adjust flight trajectory and call emission patterns in response to 34 

environmental clutter (e.g., Ding et al. , 2023; Taub and Yovel, 2020; Wheeler et al. , 2016), and 35 

alter the spatial coverage of sonar transmissions in a task-dependent manner, broadening the beam 36 

to ensure successful prey interception and narrowing the beam to assist with localization and 37 

landing (e.g., Eitan et al. , 2022; Matsuta et al. , 2013). Similarly, toothed whales modify click 38 

amplitude and inter-click intervals depending on the target range and environmental reverberation 39 

(e.g., Christman et al. , 2024; Johnson et al. , 2004; Ladegaard and Madsen, 2019; Wisniewska et al. ,40 

2012), switch from emitting single clicks to packets of clicks to detect distant targets (Finneran, 41 

2013; Ladegaard et al. , 2019), employ highly directional sonar beams to discriminate closely spaced 42 

objects (Malinka et al. , 2021), broaden the beam to ensure successful prey interception (Wisniewska 43 

et al. , 2015), and adjust their movement trajectories depending on echo ambiguity (Lee et al. , 2025). 44 

Many theoretical models have been developed to describe adaptive changes in acoustic and 45 

movement behavior during echolocation. Hand-crafted analytical models have been proposed to 46 

describe bats’ movements and sonar parameter adjustments during prey tracking and interception 47 

(e.g., Aihara et al. , 2013; Fujioka et al. , 2016; Ghose et al. , 2006; Ghose and Moss, 2006; Mansour 48 

et al. , 2019; Nguyen et al. , 2021; Nishiumi et al. , 2024; Salles et al. , 2020; Sumiya et al. , 2017). 49 

Many studies further integrated auditory perceptual cues from echoes together with spatial infor-50 

mation derived from both the bat’s self-motion and/or the coordinated movement of its sensing 51

These models emphasize the reactive aspects of sensorimotor adjustments in echolocation. The 68 

cognitive dimensions of echolocation, where the animal actively reasons about the echo-acoustic 69 

scene and determines its next set of actions after integrating information from prior echo returns 70 

(Fig. 1A), remain under-explored, even though such cognitive reasoning is often invoked when in-71 

terpreting behavioral data. For example, variations in pulse duration and inter-pulse intervals in 72 

bats, as well as adjustments of inter-click intervals in toothed whales, are commonly interpreted as 73 

evidence that these animals modulate their acoustic attention or field of view in complex environ-74 

ments. While recent deep reinforcement learning (RL) models have begun to address this gap by 75 

introducing explicit cognitive architectures to enable agents to learn actionable policies from echo 76 

returns and rewards, the interpretability of such models remains challenging (Glanois et al. , 2024). 77 

As new discoveries continue to underscore the remarkable flexibility of animal echolocation behav-78 

ior, there is a growing need for modeling frameworks that integrate the physical and physiological 79 

properties of these biological sonar systems with the associated sensory and cognitive processes to 80 

yield mechanistic insights into adaptive sonar-guided actions. 81 

“Infotaxis” has been an influential model for describing the search for odor sources in turbulent 82 

environments (Vergassola et al. , 2007), in which the agent updates an internal “world” model of 83 

probable source locations through a sensory model that encodes the likelihood of odor detection 84 

within and around a plume. The agent then chooses its next movement by maximizing the ex-85 

pected information gain at each step. While the observable behavior (agent movement following 86 

odor detection) appears similar to reactive sensorimotor models, infotaxis explicitly incorporates 87 

cognitive reasoning by specifying how sensory information should be used to update the internal 88 

world model and select the next action, providing an interpretable bridge between behavioral ob-89 

servations and model parameterization. This information-based formulation has been extended to 90 

a variety of odor sensing scenarios, including multi-agent schemes (Hajieghrary et al. , 2016; Karpas 91 

et al. , 2017; Masson et al. , 2009). The infotaxis model behavior has also been compared to the 92 

behaviors of worms, flies, mosquitoes, and moths in laboratory experiments (e.g., Calhoun et al. ,93 

2014; Hernandez-Reyes et al. , 2021; Pang et al. , 2018). In vision, similar information-based models 94 

have been proposed to model eye movements (saccades) during search and categorization tasks and 95 

compared with experimental data through summary statistics and on a fixation-by-fixation basis 96 

(Jang et al. , 2021; Najemnik and Geisler, 2005, 2009; Yang et al. , 2016). Theoretical analyses and 97 

simulations have also been used to examine the efficiency and reliability of these algorithms under 98 

realistic biological constraints, such as the energetic cost of movement (Chen et al. , 2020; Loisy 99 

and Eloy, 2022). 100 

Here, we built upon these models to frame echolocation as an active pursuit of information, 101 

extending the infotaxis algorithm to an active sensing modality that shares both similarities and 102

sensing modality in which information can be acquired from a distance. However, in olfaction 104 

and vision, even though the animals have control over their sensors [e.g., antenna movements 105 

and eye fixations (Claverie et al. , 2023; K¨ ummerer and Bethge, 2023)] to achieve better sampling 106 

and accentuate specific features in the incoming signals, they do not have control over the odor 107 

molecules or the light spectrum they receive. In contrast, echolocating animals exert active and 108 

direct control over the spectral, temporal, and directional characteristics of the transmitted signal, 109 

allowing them a greater capability to selectively sample and emphasize environmental or target 110 

features most relevant to its behavioral goals. 111 

We first developed a general Bayesian framework for echolocation that incorporates detection 112 

parameters specific to active sonar sensing. We then formulated a detailed model of an echolocating 113 

agent searching for a single target by steering a narrow sonar beam within a finite frontal search 114 

space. An important contribution of this paper is that our sensing model used a binary hypothesis 115 

test for echolocation-based target detection with sensory uncertainty captured by the probabilities 116 

of miss ( PM ) and false alarm ( PF A ), which are important intrinsic characteristics of sonar systems 117 

(Van Trees, 1968). This differs from the Poisson point process detection model in the original in-118 

fotaxis paper, where detection probabilities were based on plume concentrations (Vergassola et al. ,119 

2007). Despite its simplicity, this modeling scenario generated a variety of search behaviors, which 120 

demonstrate that the characteristic infotaxis balance between exploration and exploitation in fol-121 

lowing odor plumes also emerges in echolocation, and that the search trajectories and performance 122 

depend fundamentally on sensory information quality. Simulations further showed that infotaxis 123 

is more reliable and efficient compared with the maximum a posteriori (MAP) rule, under which 124 

the agent always directs its sonar beam to the most probable target locations. 125 

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Sec. II presents the general formulation 126 

of the echolocation infotaxis framework, a detailed formulation of the infotaxis and MAP agents 127 

in a single target search scenario, and the corresponding computational implementation. Sec. III 128 

describes typical search dynamics of the infotaxis agent, including how entropy decreases as the 129 

agent explores the search space, how sensory uncertainty influences infotaxis selection of beam 130 

aim and beam footprint size, as well as comparisons of how infotaxis and MAP searches perform. 131 

Finally, Sec. IV discusses the broader implications of our results, potential extensions of the model, 132 

and its relevance to both biological and engineered sonar systems. 133

D. Computational implementation 246 

We developed a computational implementation of the single target search scenario described 247 

above to systematically examine how an echolocating agent behaves given different action selection 248 

rules and search parameters, including sensory uncertainty PM and PF A , beam footprint size NB ,249 

search space size NA, and their relative proportion α = NB /N A. Computationally, the search 250 

space is a finite two-dimensional area represented using a hexagonal grid system (Fig. 2) defined 251 

with radius rA, and the echolocation beam footprint is a circular region with radius rB centered 252 

on the beam aim θB . Therefore, in figure annotations, rA and rB are used in place of NA and NB ,253 

respectively. A hexagonal grid is used, because in this geometry all cells along a rim at a given 254 

radius are equidistant from the center beam aim, whereas in rectangular grids, the distances would 255 

vary depending on whether the outer cells lie along an edge or at a corner. Under the hexagonal 256 

grid setup, the number of cells covered in the area within a given radius r is N = 1 + 3 r(r + 1). 257 

The agent can operate under either the infotaxis or MAP rules. Under the infotaxis rule (Eq. 6), 258 

the agent selects both the next beam aim and beam footprint radius, whereas under the MAP rule 259 

(Eq. 16) the agent selects only the next beam aim. Two sets of PM and PF A values are defined: one 260 

represents the agent’s internal model of sensory uncertainty ( P AM and P AF A ), and the other specifies 261 

the true values for the sensor in the search space ( P TM and P TF A ). This setup allows investigation 262 

of how model performance varies when the agent’s models differ from true sensory statistics. 263 

By default, the infotaxis model randomly selects the next beam aim from cells with the small-264 

est expected entropy across the entire search space, whereas the MAP model randomly selects the 265 

next beam aim from cells with the highest posterior probability. This selection can be optionally 266 

restricted to only cells within a user-defined radius from the current beam aim to make the beam 267 

aim trajectory more realistic (smoother). All simulations presented in the main text were gener-268 

ated without beam movement restriction. See Fig. S1 for search examples generated with beam 269 

movement restriction. 270

A. Infotaxis search behavior 292 

1. Typical search dynamics 293 

Given a fixed beam footprint size and moderate PF A , the infotaxis agent randomly selects beam 294 

aims from interior cells of a search space at the beginning of a search (Fig. 3A, ping 0). When 295 

the search space is moderately large relative to the beam footprint, the agent typically does not 296 

receive a target detection during the first few pings and would update the probability distribution 297 

of target location by placing higher probability in previously unexplored cells (Fig. 3A, pings 1-7). 298 

This iterative process continues until a target detection occurs, which sharply alters the distribution 299 

of target probability across the search space (Fig. 3A, ping 8). The agent now concentrates its 300 

search within and around the footprint of the ping that yielded the detection, directing subsequent 301 

beam footprints to overlap maximally with the region of high target probability (Fig. 3A, pings 302 

9-11) to further narrow down the target location. The search terminates once the target probability 303 

of a single cell exceeds the stopping threshold pth (Fig. 3F). 304 

In such a typical search sequence, the initial stage before any target detection represents the 305 

“exploration” phase, during which the agent surveys the search space to identify a probable target 306 

area. Once a detection occurs, the search transitions into the “exploitation” phase, where the 307 

agent prioritizes refining the already identified highly probable target region. As in the classic 308 

odor source tracking scenario (Vergassola et al. , 2007), an echolocating infotaxis agent naturally 309 

balances exploration and exploitation while maximizing the information gain at each ping. 310 

The initial search behavior changes dramatically when the relative beam footprint coverage α311 

is large or when PF A is high. Under these conditions, the agent exhibits a distinct boundary- or 312 

corner-seeking behavior, often directing its beam toward the edges of the search space (Fig. S2). 313 

This occurs because such beam aims reduce the number of cells covered within the footprint, as 314 

part of the beam extends outside the search space. This effectively lowers the collective probability 315 

of false alarm from its value if the beam aim was in the middle of the grid ( ≈ NB PF A , see Eq. 12), 316 

allowing the agent to be more confident about any target detection. 317

3. Impacts of sensory uncertainty 338 

When misses or false alarms occur, the infotaxis agent recovers from these sensory errors in 339 

distinctive ways that impact the search efficiency. Following a false alarm ( X = 1 despite the 340 

target being outside the footprint), the agent directs the next few pings to sequentially investigate 341 

cells covered by the beam footprint yielding the erroneous detection, and efficiently rules out the 342 

possibility that the target is located withing these cells (Fig. 3D, ping 4-6). It follows intuitively 343 

that the recovery time after a false alarm depends on the size of the beam footprint. Because a 344 

target detection—whether true or spurious—causes a sharp contraction of highly probable target 345 

region, followed by a gradual redistribution of target probability, a false alarm appears as a distinct 346 

“dip” in the actual entropy curve of a search (Fig. 3I, between ping 2-6). 347 

In contrast, the infotaxis agent is affected more severely by a miss, often for a longer duration. 348 

When a miss occurs ( X = 0 despite the target being within the beam footprint), the agent assigns 349 

low target probability to the cells covered by the beam and moves away from the area, as revisiting 350 

it is deemed less informative (Fig. 3E, ping 3, low expected entropy around the beam footprint). 351 

The agent typically revisits the missed region only after all other parts of the search space have been 352 

explored without success (Fig. 3E, pings 4-19). Therefore, when a miss occurs early in a search, the 353 

resulting entropy curve resembles that of searches with a long exploration phase (Fig. 3J). Once 354 

the agent realizes that a miss has occurred after failing to detect the target elsewhere, the entropy 355 

rises abruptly to the level observed before the miss, reflecting renewed uncertainty and resumption 356

IV. DISCUSSION 563 

In this paper, we extended infotaxis, a cognitive Bayesian modeling framework, to echolocation, 564 

an active sensing modality in which the agent actively moves and emits sound to acquire new infor-565 

mation from echoes (Fig. 1). Through theoretical formulation and computational simulations, we 566 

examined how the target search behavior of an echolocating infotaxis agent varies with important 567

combinations, revealing how decision rules interact with sonar system properties to shape search 571

Our results show that the characteristic infotaxis balance between exploration and exploita-573 

tion observed in olfactory sensing also emerges under echolocation. In olfactory search, the agent 574 

chooses between moving to a neighboring location or remaining stationary to maximize the ex-575 

pected reduction in entropy of the search scene (Vergassola et al. , 2007). In echolocation search, 576 

the agent chooses where to aim its sonar beam, either revisiting cells already covered by previ-577 

ous pings or exploring a previously unobserved area, and can select among different sonar beam 578 

footprint sizes (Sec. II B). This trade-off is evident when considering agent behavior under sensory 579 

uncertainty: Directing the beam to cover previously unobserved cells allows the agent to rapidly 580 

identify a probable target region, but slows detection if the target was missed in earlier echo returns; 581 

repeating the same beam aim allows the agent to confirm the validity of a target detection, but 582 

does not improve localization (Fig. 3A, 3D, 3E and Fig. 8). Similarly, using a large beam footprint 583 

enables quick survey of the search space, but provides limited information on the precise target 584 

location (Fig. 3B-3C). Importantly, accumulation of evidence remains a fundamental property of 585 

the echolocation infotaxis model, in which the binary hypothesis test observation model gives rise 586 

to a spatially graded information landscape that supports the exploration-exploitation trade-off. 587 

Indeed, the posterior distribution of target location continues to update even under consecutive 588 

pings with the same beam aim, a scenario analogous to the agent remaining stationary in olfactory 589 

sensing. 590 

Our analytical and simulation results also show that the probabilities of false alarm and miss 591 

influence the infotaxis agent behavior in distinct ways. In the single target search context, PF A has 592 

a much stronger impact on action selection than PM . When PF A is high, the uncertainty about 593 

whether a detection is true drives the infotaxis agent to repeat the same beam aim after receiving a 594 

detection (Fig. 8) and to favor beam positions that cover fewer cells (Fig. S2), thereby minimizing 595 

the likelihood of false alarms. This is because even though PF A is defined for each individual cell, 596 

the agent experiences a combined effective false alarm rate from all cells covered within the beam 597 

footprint (see Eq. 11-12). However, the cost of misses in overall search length is far greater than 598 

the cost of false alarms: The infotaxis agent can recover from false alarms efficiently within a few 599 

pings (Fig. 3D and 3I) but cannot recognize a miss until it has explored all other cells in the search 600 

space (Fig. 3E and 3J). These differences mirror real-world sonar operational consequences, where 601 

false alarms prompt immediate actions and missed detections often incur substantially higher costs 602 

(Urick, 1983). 603

The information-based nature of infotaxis becomes particularly apparent when compared with 614

Motor actions define the space of behavioral choices available to an agent to achieve its task 648 

goal. These actions correspond to measurable observables in behavioral experiments, and thus form 649

by physical and physiological limitations to ensure modeled behavioral outputs remain biologically 656 

realistic. 657

a distal sensing modality in which adjustments of beam aim, beam coverage, and transmission 678 

rate likely incur lower energetic costs than in proximal sensing modalities such as tactile sensing, 679 

and electrolocation, which has a substantially smaller sensing volume than echolocation (Nelson 680

Even though we were not able to identify existing behavioral datasets suitable for direct com-683 

parison with the single target search model presented here, we have conducted controlled robotic 684 

experiments showing successful infotaxis-based sonar target localization in a one-dimensional search 685 

space under varying beamwidth conditions (Mboya et al. ) and mismatched sensory uncertainty 686 

(Mudassir et al. ). These studies provide initial validation of echolocation infotaxis on a robotic 687 

platform and motivate future tests using animal behavioral experiments. Such model-data compar-688 

isons will require mapping the abstract model parameters presented here to specific experimental 689 

contexts. For example, beam footprint size is species-specific and often varies dynamically depend-690 

ing on task priorities (e.g., Jensen et al. , 2015; Motoi et al. , 2017), whereas sensory uncertainty is 691 

further tied to intrinsic target scattering properties, which influence the animal’s ability to detect 692 

or discriminate targets (e.g., Wei et al. , 2021). 693 

The echolocation infotaxis model presented in this paper differs in setup and objectives from 694 

deep RL models of echolocating bats (Mohan and Vanderelst, 2020; Nguyen and Vanderelst, 2022, 695 

2025), which capture similar cognitive and sensorimotor aspects of decision making in echolocation. 696 

To investigate intrinsic properties of infotaxis search in echolocation, our model is intentionally ab-697 

stract: the complexity and uncertainty of auditory scene analysis and target echo detection are 698 

encapsulated by PM and PF A , whose influence on action selection can be explicitly traced. This 699 

simplified observation model was chosen deliberately to create a tractable framework in which the 700 

resulting decision behavior could be analyzed analytically and computationally. In contrast, the 701 

deep RL models operate directly on simulated echo time series and learn action policies through 702 

trial-and-error to maximize cumulative reward. The infotaxis agent maintains an explicit proba-703 

bilistic world model tied to the task goal (e.g., a distribution over target location in search tasks or 704 

target category in discrimination tasks), whereas in RL-based approaches, task-relevant informa-705 

tion is encoded within learned policy or value representations. Both infotaxis and RL approaches 706 

generally require reformulation when the task context changes. RL approaches typically require 707 

retraining after such reformulation, whereas infotaxis action selection is determined directly from 708 

the probabilistic model and objective function. Selecting actions in infotaxis can also become com-709 

putationally expensive when the motor action space is large, as each decision involves evaluating 710 

the expected entropy over all possible action combinations. In comparison, although RL train-711

In summary, we developed an infotaxis model for echolocation that integrates sensory uncer-719 

tainty, motor action, and information-based reasoning in a single interpretable framework. Im-720

and efficient than a reflexive MAP agent, though its performance declined as sensory informa-722

echolocation and active sonar contexts, such as multi-target search among clutter. More broadly, 724 

by incorporating experimentally derived sensorimotor capabilities into a cognitive model, infotaxis 725 

can provide interpretable linkages between behavioral observables, sensory mechanisms, and deci-726 

sion making processes. We propose that infotaxis offers a powerful framework for iterative model 727 

refinement and experimental testing that can help model and understand sonar-guided autonomy 728 

in natural and engineered systems. 729
